📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Historical Figures Overview

Benjamin Franklin (1706-1790): Founding Father, scientist, inventor, author. Known for experiments with electricity, invented bifocals and the Franklin stove. Helped draft the Declaration of Independence.

Thomas Jefferson (1743-1826): Third U.S. President, primary author of the Declaration of Independence. Champion of individual rights and education.

Lewis and Clark (1804-1806): Led the Corps of Discovery expedition from St. Louis to the Pacific Ocean, mapping the American West.

Historical Figures Continued

Harriet Tubman (c.1822-1913): Escaped slave who became a conductor on the Underground Railroad, leading approximately 70 people to freedom. Later served as a Union spy during the Civil War.

Theodore Roosevelt (1858-1919): 26th President, conservationist who established national parks. Known for his "Rough Riders" and the Panama Canal.

George Washington Carver (c.1864-1943): Agricultural scientist who developed hundreds of products from peanuts, sweet potatoes, and other crops. Promoted crop rotation to help Southern farmers.

Geography Concepts

Geographic Features: Mountains, rivers, lakes, plains, deserts, coastlines

Cultural Characteristics: Language, food, clothing, holidays, traditions

Human-Environment Interaction: Building homes, farming, creating roads, using natural resources

Location Vocabulary: City, county, state, nation, continent, hemisphere

Economics Concepts

Scarcity: We can't have everything we want, so we must make choices.

Income: Money earned from working (wages).

Spending: Using money to buy goods and services.

Saving: Setting aside money for future use.

Opportunity Cost: What you give up when you make a choice.

🔍 Inquiry-Based Lessons

🔬 Inquiry 1: What Makes Someone a Hero?

Compelling Question: What makes someone a hero in American history?

Materials: Biographies of historical figures, chart paper, hero attribute cards
  1. Engage: Ask students to name people they think are heroes. What makes them heroic?
  2. Explore: In small groups, read about one historical figure and identify their heroic actions.
  3. Explain: Create a class chart of "hero qualities" based on findings.
  4. Elaborate: Students draw and write about a historical figure using hero qualities.
  5. Evaluate: Present heroes and explain why they're important to American history.

Standards: SS1H1

🗺️ Inquiry 2: How Does Where We Live Shape How We Live?

Compelling Question: How does geography affect the way people live?

  1. Engage: Show photos of homes in different environments. How are they different?
  2. Explore: Compare living in mountains, near oceans, in deserts, and on plains.
  3. Explain: Discuss how people adapt their homes, clothes, and food based on location.
  4. Elaborate: Design a home for a specific environment.
  5. Evaluate: Explain how the home fits the environment.

Standards: SS1G1, SS1G3

💰 Inquiry 3: Why Can't We Have Everything We Want?

Compelling Question: Why do we have to make choices about what to buy?

  1. Engage: Give students a pretend $10. Show items costing more than $10 total. What do they do?
  2. Explore: Discuss why we can't buy everything (scarcity).
  3. Explain: Introduce opportunity cost - what you give up to get something else.
  4. Elaborate: Role-play saving vs. spending decisions.
  5. Evaluate: Write/draw about a choice and what was given up.

Standards: SS1E1, SS1E3

📰 Student Reading Passages

📖 Benjamin Franklin

Benjamin Franklin was a great American. He was an inventor. He made bifocals to help people see. He did experiments with lightning. He helped write important papers for our country. Benjamin Franklin is on the $100 bill!

Reading Level: 1st Grade | Standards: SS1H1

📖 Harriet Tubman

Harriet Tubman was very brave. She escaped from slavery. Then she went back many times to help others escape. She used the Underground Railroad. This was not a real train. It was a secret path to freedom. Harriet Tubman is an American hero.

Reading Level: 1st Grade | Standards: SS1H1

📖 Where Do You Live?

You live in a city or town. Your city is in a county. Your county is in the state of Georgia. Georgia is in the United States of America. The United States is in North America. North America is a continent. You can find all of these on a map!

Reading Level: 1st Grade | Standards: SS1G2

📖 Saving and Spending

When you get money, you have a choice. You can spend it now or save it for later. If you save, you can buy something bigger later. If you spend, you get something now. Both choices are okay! It's important to think about what you really want.

Reading Level: 1st Grade | Standards: SS1E3

🎯 Differentiation Strategies

English Language Learners (ELL)

  • Provide picture cards with vocabulary words and definitions
  • Use sentence frames: "Benjamin Franklin invented _____."
  • Partner with English-proficient peers for activities
  • Create visual timelines for historical figures
  • Use maps with labels in home language when possible

Gifted and Advanced Learners

  • Research additional historical figures and compare contributions
  • Create a "What would happen if..." scenario for historical events
  • Design an invention to solve a modern problem
  • Calculate more complex spending/saving scenarios
  • Compare maps from different time periods

Students with Learning Differences

  • Provide audio recordings of reading passages
  • Use manipulatives for economic concepts (play money, tokens)
  • Break biographical information into smaller chunks
  • Offer choice in how to demonstrate learning
  • Provide graphic organizers with partial completion

💡 Common Misconceptions

Historical Figures

❌ Misconception: Benjamin Franklin was a President

✅ Accurate: Franklin was never President. He was a Founding Father, inventor, and diplomat, but he never held the office of President.

❌ Misconception: The Underground Railroad was an actual train

✅ Accurate: The Underground Railroad was a secret network of people and safe houses that helped enslaved people escape to freedom. There were no actual trains.

Geography

❌ Misconception: States and countries are the same thing

✅ Accurate: States are parts of a country. Georgia is a state that is part of the country called the United States of America.

Economics

❌ Misconception: Money comes from the bank whenever you need it

✅ Accurate: People earn money by working. The bank holds money that people have already earned and saved.

❌ Misconception: Saving means you can never spend the money

✅ Accurate: Saving means setting money aside for later. You can still spend it, but you're waiting to spend it on something you really want or need.

🌎 Cultural Connections

Diverse Perspectives on Historical Figures

  • Include stories of lesser-known historical figures from diverse backgrounds

  • Connect to students' own cultural heroes and role models

Geography and Cultural Diversity

When teaching about geographic features and how people adapt:

  • Show examples of homes from different cultures and climates
  • Discuss how students' families might have adapted from their home countries
  • Compare food, clothing, and traditions in different geographic areas

Economics Across Cultures

  • Discuss how different cultures think about saving and spending
  • Explore bartering traditions from various cultures
  • Connect to family values around money and work

🧒 AI Tools for Students

⚠️ First Grade AI Safety Guidelines

First grade students should only use AI tools with direct teacher supervision. All activities below are designed for teacher-led, whole-class experiences. Students should never independently access AI tools at this age.

Teacher-Led Activity

🎨 Meet a Historical Figure

How it works: Teacher uses AI to have a "conversation" with a historical figure while students ask questions.

  • Benjamin Franklin Interview: "What was it like to experiment with lightning?"
  • Harriet Tubman Story: "How did you help people escape to freedom?"
  • Lewis and Clark Adventure: "What did you discover on your journey?"

Learning Focus: Students see AI can simulate conversations, but teacher verifies historical accuracy.

Standards: SS1H1

Teacher-Led Activity

🗺️ Virtual Geography Explorer

How it works: Class uses AI-assisted mapping or images to explore different places.

  • Find Our State: "Show us where Georgia is on the map!"
  • Compare Places: "What does a desert look like? What about mountains?"
  • Explore Continents: "Let's see what North America looks like from above."

Standards: SS1G1, SS1G2

Teacher-Led Activity

💰 Money Choices Helper

How it works: Teacher presents spending/saving scenarios and uses AI to generate outcomes.

  • Scenario Generator: "What happens if I save my money for 3 weeks?"
  • Choice Comparison: "Help us compare buying a toy now vs. saving for a bigger toy."
  • Needs vs. Wants: Generate pictures of items for sorting activities.

Standards: SS1E1, SS1E3

Teacher-Led Activity

📖 Story Creation with Historical Figures

How it works: Class collaboratively creates a story about a historical figure with AI assistance.

  • "Let's write a story about Benjamin Franklin inventing something new!"
  • "What adventure might Theodore Roosevelt have in a national park?"
  • Students contribute ideas while teacher types and reads AI responses.

Standards: SS1H1

Age-Appropriate AI Concepts for First Grade

  • 🤖 "The computer can pretend to be a historical figure, but it's not really them."
  • 📚 "AI learned from reading lots of books, just like we're learning to read!"
  • ✅ "We always check if the computer's answers match what we learned."
  • 👩‍🏫 "The teacher helps us know if the computer is right or wrong."

Building Digital Citizenship

  • 👨‍👩‍👧 Always use computers with a teacher or grown-up
  • 🤔 Think about whether information makes sense
  • ❓ Ask questions if something seems wrong
  • 🔒 Never share personal information with computers
