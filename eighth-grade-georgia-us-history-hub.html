📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Reconstruction & New South (1865-1900)

Reconstruction: Federal troops occupied South. Freedmen's Bureau helped formerly enslaved people. 13th, 14th, 15th Amendments passed. African Americans gained political power briefly.

End of Reconstruction: Compromise of 1877 withdrew federal troops. Rise of Jim Crow laws enforced segregation. Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) established "separate but equal."

New South: Henry Grady promoted industrialization. Growth of textile mills, railroads. Atlanta became regional center. But sharecropping trapped many in poverty.

Civil Rights Movement

Georgia Leaders: Martin Luther King Jr. (Atlanta native), John Lewis, Andrew Young, Julian Bond.

Key Events: Albany Movement (1961-62), Atlanta student sit-ins, March on Washington (1963), Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965).

Georgia Milestones: Integration of University of Georgia (1961), Charlayne Hunter and Hamilton Holmes. Atlanta "city too busy to hate."

Modern Georgia

Economic Growth: Atlanta as transportation hub. Hartsfield-Jackson busiest airport. Headquarters of Coca-Cola, Home Depot, Delta, UPS.

Political Changes: Jimmy Carter elected governor (1970), president (1976). Maynard Jackson first Black mayor of major Southern city (1973).

Demographics: Rapid population growth, increasing diversity, suburban expansion.

🔍 Inquiry-Based Lessons

✊ Inquiry 1: Was Nonviolent Resistance the Best Strategy?

Compelling Question: Should civil rights activists have used only nonviolent methods?

  1. Engage: Show images of sit-ins and marches alongside Black Power movement.
  2. Explore: Research different civil rights strategies: MLK's nonviolence, Malcolm X's self-defense, Black Panthers.
  3. Explain: Analyze effectiveness and consequences of different approaches.
  4. Elaborate: Write position papers defending one approach.
  5. Evaluate: Socratic seminar on strategies.

Standards: SS8H10-11

🏗️ Inquiry 2: Did the New Deal Help or Hurt Georgia?

Compelling Question: Was the New Deal good for all Georgians?

  1. Engage: Show images of Georgia during Depression - rural poverty, Atlanta growth.
  2. Explore: Research New Deal programs' effects on different groups: farmers, workers, African Americans.
  3. Explain: Analyze who benefited and who was left out.
  4. Elaborate: Create oral histories from different perspectives.
  5. Evaluate: Write editorials evaluating New Deal's legacy.

Standards: SS8H8

🔬 Document-Based Investigations

Investigation 1: Jim Crow Laws

Primary Source: Georgia segregation statutes

  • What activities did these laws regulate?
  • How did these laws affect daily life?
  • What was the purpose of such detailed separation?

Investigation 2: "Letter from Birmingham Jail"

Primary Source: MLK's letter (excerpts)

  • Why did King write from jail?
  • How did he justify breaking unjust laws?
  • Why does King express disappointment with "white moderates"?

Investigation 3: 1996 Olympics

Primary Sources: News coverage, city planning documents

  • How did Atlanta prepare for the Olympics?
  • What were the benefits and costs?
  • How did the Olympics change Atlanta?

📰 Student Reading Passages

📖 The Rise of Jim Crow

After Reconstruction ended in 1877, Southern states passed laws to keep Black and white people separate. These "Jim Crow" laws affected every part of life - schools, restaurants, theaters, trains, even drinking fountains. In Georgia, segregation was strictly enforced. Black citizens who challenged these laws faced arrest, violence, or worse. The Supreme Court's Plessy v. Ferguson decision (1896) declared segregation legal as long as facilities were "separate but equal." In reality, facilities for Black Americans were always inferior.

Standards: SS8H6

📖 Atlanta's Civil Rights Movement

Atlanta became a center of the Civil Rights Movement. Martin Luther King Jr. grew up here, and his church, Ebenezer Baptist, became a meeting place for activists. In 1960, Atlanta University students organized sit-ins at downtown lunch counters. Unlike other Southern cities, Atlanta's business leaders negotiated with protesters. Mayor William Hartsfield called Atlanta "the city too busy to hate." While this wasn't entirely true - segregation remained - Atlanta avoided the worst violence seen elsewhere.

Standards: SS8H10-11

💡 Common Misconceptions

❌ Slavery ended, so African Americans were immediately equal

✅ Accurate: Jim Crow laws, violence, and economic exploitation continued to oppress Black Americans for another century after emancipation.

❌ The Civil Rights Movement was only about MLK

✅ Accurate: Thousands of ordinary people, local leaders, and various organizations contributed to the movement. Many Georgia activists made crucial contributions.

❌ Civil rights issues were resolved in the 1960s

✅ Accurate: While landmark legislation was passed, issues of racial inequality continue today. Understanding this history helps us address ongoing challenges.
