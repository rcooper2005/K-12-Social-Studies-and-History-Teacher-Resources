📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Georgia Historical Figures

James Oglethorpe (1696-1785): Founder of the Georgia colony in 1733. Sought to create a haven for debtors and establish a buffer zone between Spanish Florida and English colonies.

Tomochichi (c.1644-1739): Chief of the Yamacraw people. Welcomed Oglethorpe and helped establish peaceful relations between Native Americans and colonists.

Mary Musgrove (c.1700-1765): Creek-English interpreter who served as liaison between Native Americans and English colonists. Essential to Georgia's early success.

Georgia Historical Figures Continued

Sequoyah (c.1770-1843): Created the Cherokee syllabary, allowing the Cherokee language to be written and read. Lived in Georgia before removal.

Jackie Robinson (1919-1972): Born in Cairo, Georgia. First African American to play in Major League Baseball in the modern era (1947).

Martin Luther King Jr. (1929-1968): Born in Atlanta. Led the civil rights movement through nonviolent resistance.

Jimmy Carter (1924-present): 39th President, from Plains, Georgia. Won Nobel Peace Prize for humanitarian work.

John Lewis (1940-2020): Civil rights leader from Troy, Alabama. U.S. Congressman representing Georgia for over 30 years.

Georgia Geography

Blue Ridge Mountains: Northern Georgia, part of Appalachian chain. Highest point: Brasstown Bald.

Piedmont: Rolling hills between mountains and coastal plain. Atlanta located here.

Coastal Plain: Largest region, flat land with rivers and swamps.

Major Rivers: Chattahoochee, Savannah, Altamaha, Flint, Oconee

Three Branches of Government

Legislative Branch (Congress): Makes laws. Includes Senate and House of Representatives.

Executive Branch (President): Carries out and enforces laws. Includes the Cabinet.

Judicial Branch (Supreme Court): Interprets laws and determines if they follow the Constitution.

🔍 Inquiry-Based Lessons

🍑 Inquiry 1: How Did People Work Together to Build Georgia?

Compelling Question: How did different people contribute to Georgia's founding?

Materials: Biographies of Oglethorpe, Tomochichi, and Mary Musgrove; Venn diagrams; timeline materials
  1. Engage: Show a map of early Georgia. Ask: "Who lived here first? Who came later?"
  2. Explore: Small groups research one historical figure and their contributions.
  3. Explain: Share findings. Discuss how cooperation helped Georgia succeed.
  4. Elaborate: Create a class mural showing how the three worked together.
  5. Evaluate: Write/draw about how cooperation helps communities.

Standards: SS2H1, SS2G2

🏛️ Inquiry 2: How Do the Three Branches Keep Our Government Fair?

Compelling Question: Why do we need three branches of government?

  1. Engage: Role-play: What if one person made all the rules?
  2. Explore: Learn about each branch through picture books and videos.
  3. Explain: Discuss checks and balances in simple terms.
  4. Elaborate: Create a three-branches mobile or poster.
  5. Evaluate: Match jobs to branches in an assessment.

Standards: SS2CG2

💰 Inquiry 3: Why Do Communities Trade?

Compelling Question: Why can't one community make everything it needs?

  1. Engage: Ask: "What do we have in Georgia that other places might want?"
  2. Explore: Map Georgia products and discuss what we get from other places.
  3. Explain: Discuss specialization and trade benefits.
  4. Elaborate: Simulation: communities trading "products" with each other.
  5. Evaluate: Explain why trade helps everyone.

Standards: SS2E1, SS2E2

📰 Student Reading Passages

📖 James Oglethorpe

James Oglethorpe founded the colony of Georgia in 1733. He wanted to give poor people from England a new start. He met Tomochichi, a Native American chief. Mary Musgrove helped them talk to each other. Together, they built the city of Savannah.

Standards: SS2H1

📖 Martin Luther King Jr.

Martin Luther King Jr. was born in Atlanta, Georgia. He believed all people should be treated fairly. He led peaceful marches and gave famous speeches. His most famous speech was "I Have a Dream." He worked hard so everyone would have equal rights.

Standards: SS2H1

📖 Our Three Branches

Our government has three parts called branches. Congress makes the laws. The President makes sure the laws are followed. The Supreme Court decides if laws are fair. Having three branches keeps our government balanced. No one branch has too much power.

Standards: SS2CG2

💡 Common Misconceptions

Georgia History

❌ Misconception: Georgia was empty before Oglethorpe arrived

✅ Accurate: Native Americans, including the Yamacraw led by Tomochichi, had lived in the area for thousands of years before European colonization.

Government

❌ Misconception: The President makes all the laws

✅ Accurate: Congress (the legislative branch) makes laws. The President signs them into law or vetoes them, and ensures they are carried out.

Economics

❌ Misconception: Banks give away free money

✅ Accurate: Banks keep money safe that people have deposited. They may lend money, but it must be paid back with interest.
