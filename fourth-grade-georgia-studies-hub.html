📖 Teacher Background Knowledge

Prehistoric Georgia & Native Americans

Mississippian Culture (800-1600 CE): Built mound complexes like Etowah and Ocmulgee. Developed agriculture, complex societies, and trade networks.

Cherokee Nation: Lived in north Georgia. Developed written language (Sequoyah's syllabary). Forced removal on Trail of Tears (1838).

Creek (Muscogee) Nation: Lived in central and south Georgia. Important in colonial trade. Also removed in 1830s.

Colonial Georgia

Founding (1733): James Oglethorpe established Georgia as last of 13 colonies. Designed as buffer between Spanish Florida and Carolina colonies.

Key Figures: Tomochichi (Yamacraw chief), Mary Musgrove (interpreter), Salzburgers (German Protestant refugees).

Early Economy: Originally prohibited slavery; restriction lifted 1751. Shifted to plantation economy.

Civil War & Reconstruction

Georgia in Civil War: Confederate state. Sherman's March to the Sea (1864) devastated central Georgia.

Reconstruction: Military occupation, freedmen's rights, new constitution. Ended with Compromise of 1877.

New South: Henry Grady promoted industrialization. Jim Crow laws established segregation.

Civil Rights Movement

Key Figures: Martin Luther King Jr. (Atlanta native), John Lewis, Andrew Young.

Events: Atlanta student sit-ins (1960), Albany Movement (1961-62), integration of University of Georgia.

Legacy: Voting Rights Act (1965), election of first African American mayors.

🔍 Inquiry-Based Lessons

🪶 Inquiry 1: Was the Trail of Tears Avoidable?

Compelling Question: Could the Cherokee have remained in Georgia?

  1. Engage: Show map of Cherokee Nation in Georgia. Ask: What happened to these people?
  2. Explore: Research Cherokee adaptations, Supreme Court case, and Andrew Jackson's response.
  3. Explain: Analyze multiple perspectives: Cherokee, settlers, state/federal government.
  4. Elaborate: Write diary entries from different perspectives.
  5. Evaluate: Debate whether removal was inevitable or preventable.

Standards: SS4H4

⚔️ Inquiry 2: How Did Georgia Change After the Civil War?

Compelling Question: Was life better or worse for Georgians after the Civil War?

  1. Engage: Compare photos of Georgia before and after Sherman's March.
  2. Explore: Research Reconstruction from perspectives of freedmen, former Confederates, and northern politicians.
  3. Explain: Create before/after comparison charts for different groups.
  4. Elaborate: Design a newspaper front page from 1875 Georgia.
  5. Evaluate: Argue whether Reconstruction succeeded or failed.

Standards: SS4H5

🔬 Document-Based Investigations

Primary Source: Excerpts from Cherokee Phoenix (first Native American newspaper)

  • Why was having a newspaper important to the Cherokee?
  • What issues did the Cherokee write about?
  • How does this challenge stereotypes about Native Americans?

Investigation 3: Sherman's Letters

Primary Source: Excerpts from Sherman's communications

  • What was Sherman's strategy and purpose?
  • How did this affect Georgia civilians?
  • Was this warfare justified?

📰 Student Reading Passages

📖 The Founding of Georgia

In 1733, James Oglethorpe sailed to America with a plan. He wanted to start a new colony where poor people from England could have a fresh start. When his ship landed at a bluff on the Savannah River, he met Tomochichi, chief of the Yamacraw people. With help from interpreter Mary Musgrove, the two leaders made an agreement. The colonists could settle on the land, and the Yamacraw would be their allies. Oglethorpe named the new colony Georgia after King George II.

Standards: SS4H2

📖 The Trail of Tears

The Cherokee had lived in Georgia for hundreds of years. They had their own government, newspaper, and written language. But when gold was discovered in north Georgia, the state wanted their land. Even though the Supreme Court said Georgia couldn't take Cherokee land, President Andrew Jackson refused to enforce the ruling. In 1838, soldiers forced nearly 16,000 Cherokee to march to Oklahoma. About 4,000 died on the journey, which is why it's called the Trail of Tears.

Standards: SS4H4

💡 Common Misconceptions

❌ Georgia was founded as a place for criminals

✅ Accurate: Georgia was founded for the "worthy poor" - debtors who owed money, not criminals.

❌ The Cherokee were "uncivilized" before removal

✅ Accurate: The Cherokee had a written language, constitution, newspaper, and organized government.

❌ Reconstruction was a complete failure

✅ Accurate: Reconstruction established public schools, gave voting rights to African Americans, and passed important constitutional amendments, though many gains were later reversed.
